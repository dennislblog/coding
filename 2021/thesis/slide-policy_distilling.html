# Universal Trading for Order Execution with Oracle Policy Distillation

Summary

High noise-signal ratio and imperfect market information made it difficult to build up sample efficient reinforcement learning

In this paper, they leverage a policy distillation method to better guide the learning process by an oracle teacher with perfect market information.

The extensive experiments have shown significant improvements of our method over various strong baselines, with reasonable trading actions.

1 / 7

# Introduction

  • Scope: order execution in multi-assets portfolio management problem domain
  • As discussed in (Cartea, Jaimungal, and Penalva 2015), the main challenge of order execution lies in a trade-off between avoiding market impact and restraining opportunity
  • Previously, traditional analytical solutions rely on stringent market price assumptions (e.g., price movements, volumne variance, etc.)
  • Model-free methods have been proposed to optimize execution strategy through interacting with the market environment purely based on the market data
2 / 7

# Objective

  • RL solutions may suffer from a vital issue, i.e., the largely noisy and imperfect information. For one thing, the noisy data (Wu, Gattami, and Flierl 2020) could lead to a quite low sample efficiency of RL methods
  • the only information that can be utilized when taking actions is the historical market information, without any obvious clues about the future market trends

Previous works of policy distillation primarily focus on multi-task learning or model compression, which aims at deriving a comprehensive or tiny policy to behave normally in different game environments. Their work dedicates to bridge the gap between the imperfect market states and the optimal sequential decisions using the teacher-student learning paradigm, which has not been properly stutied before

3 / 7

# Review

  • It is originally proposed in (Bertsimas and Lo 1998) where the market price follows an arithmetic random walk in the presence of linear price impact function of the trading behavior
  • Almgren & Chriss (2001) extended the above solution and incorporated both temporary and permanent price impact with market price following a Brownian motion process
  • Several extensions to these pioneering works have been proposed in the past decades (Cartea and Jaimungal 2016; Gueant and Lehalle 2015; Gueant 2016; Casgrain and Jaimungal 2019) with modeling of a variety of market features.
  • From the mentioned literature, several closed-form solutions have been derived based on stringent market assumptions. However, they might not be practical in real-world situations thus it appeals to non-parametric solutions (Ning, Ling, and Jaimungal 2018).
  • Several RL solutions have been proposed to improve over the anlytical solutions (Daberius, Granat, and Karlsson 2019; Hendricks and Wilcox 2014), or test the validity of MDP under the imposed market assumptions (Hu 2016).
  • However, these RL-based methods still rely on financial model assumption of the market dynamics thus lack of practical value
  • Another stream of RL methods abandon these financial market assumptions and utilize model-free RL to optimize execution strategy (Nevmyvaka, Feng, and Kearns 2006; Ning, Ling, and Jaimungal 2018; Lin and Beling 2020).
  • These methods face challenges of utilizing imperfect and yet noisy market information. They are also inefficient in the sense that each policy of different instruments are individually trained.
