# Introduction

VWAP is a commonly used benchmark to judge the execution quality of a strategy

  • In the United States, survey data in a report by Bank of America (2007) indicate that VWAP orders represent close to 50% of all trading by institutional investors
  • Almgren et al., (2005) report that VWAP orders for individual stocks represent 16% of all trades
  • Domowitz and Yegerman (2011) report that when trying to place an order that ranges from 0.5% to 5% of daily market volume between 23% and 27% of all algorithmic trades follow VWAP strategies
  • Australian Securities Exchange (2010) estimates that 32.3% of buy-side algorithmic trades are VWAP orders
  • End-of-day VWAP for individual stocks is reported by exchanges, and guaranteed VWAP orders are available through most brokerage houses

static vs dynamic

  • determined before trading opens for the day
  • trying to match the volume profile throughout the day dynamically

We test the robustness of our results in two ways.

  • stock prices are martingales, conditional on realized market volume, however, this assumption may fail to hold in real case. So they compare strategies with and without assuming this assumption (assumption-binding strategy is better)
  • we compare five alternative strategies proposed in the literature to track VWAP, to the strategies we develop in this article (theirs are better, I thought robustness meaning little change of parameter with rolling base estimation)

provide optimal trading strategies for a general model of stock price and trading volume dynamics for three different specifications of transaction costs associated with trading

  • transaction costs are constant
  • transaction costs increase linearly with the number of shares traded
  • we consider a nonlinear model of market impact that also allows trades to permanently affect the price of the stock (static trading policy)

we test the performance of the proposed VWAP tracking strategies on the actual TAQ data, not data simulated from the estimated model

# Literature on VWAP tracking strategies is relatively recent.

  • Madhavan (2002) discusses various types of VWAP strategies, their advantages and disadvantages, and challenges encountered when implementing a strategy to track VWAP
  • Konishi (2002) derives a static optimal execution strategy for tracking VWAP
  • McCulloch and Kazakov (2012) extend the model of Konishi (2002) to the case where the stock price is not a martingale and show that the mean-variance optimal VWAP trading strategy is the sum of a minimum variance strategy and a directional pricing strategy
  • Białkowski et al. (2008) and Humphery-Jenner (2011) suggest using an adaptive, fixed-schedule, trading strategy, with trades adjusted to match the expected market volume over the remaining time intervals.
  • Frei and Westray (2015) consider a continuous-time model and offer an algorithm to construct the optimal trading strategy that minimizes a weighted sum of the difference between personal and market VWAP and the variance of the difference, in a model where volume is assumed to be known in advance of trading, and where the trader faces execution slippage costs
  • Cartea and Jaimungal (2016a) consider the problem of tracking VWAP accounting for price pressure created by imbalances in the net order flow and find an optimal execution strategy that adjusts trading based on trade flow imbalances
  • Literatures show the positive dependence between trading volume and the variance of stock returns (their model incorporate these features using 18 months of DJI300 TAQ data)
  • Although several papers in the VWAP literature consider models of stochastic volume, to the best of our knowledge, this article is the first to find an optimal strategy to match VWAP under a model of stochastic volume that affects price dynamics
  • Hasbrouck (1991), Hausman et al. (1992), and Keim and Madhavan (1996), using different data sets and methodologies, find that, empirically, the impact of a trade on the stock price is a concave function of trade size
  • Estimating market impact from trades empirically is also discussed in Almgren et al. (2005), Obizhaeva and Wang (2006), Engle et al. (2008), Gatheral et al. (2012), and reviewed in Gatheral and Schied (2013).
  • Our model for temporary impact follows the model in Almgren et al. (2005), and, consistent with the empirical literature, assumes a nonlinear, concave, impact function. Our model also preserve assumptions that as market volume increases, volatility increases and bid-ask spreads decrease
